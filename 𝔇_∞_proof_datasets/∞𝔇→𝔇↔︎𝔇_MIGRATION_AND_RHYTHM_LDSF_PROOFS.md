## Purpose

This document provides **publish-ready empirical anchors** for classifying **migration and rhythmic systems** as a **shared, externally measurable field (LDSF)** by mapping:

**Claim ‚Üí measurable variables ‚Üí international datasets ‚Üí measurement methods ‚Üí falsifiability**

## LDSF Claim Being Validated (Empirical / Agnostic)

> **Claim:**  
> Migration and biological rhythmic systems constitute **observer-independent shared fields**: measurable, reproducible patterns in timing, phase, periodicity, route structure, and population synchrony that persist beyond individuals and can be independently verified across observers, instruments, and institutions.

---

## Minimal Variable Set Required for LDSF Classification

The following variables are sufficient to test ‚Äúshared field‚Äù status with strict falsifiability:

### A) Rhythm variables (Chronobiology)
- period (hours for circadian; days for infradian; ~annual for circannual)
- phase (clock time of peak activity / physiology; e.g., onset times)
- amplitude (activity counts, hormone concentrations, expression counts)
- entrainment sensitivity (phase response to light/temp/food/season cues)
- free-running deviation under constant conditions (no Zeitgeber)

### B) Migration variables (Movement ecology)
- individual tracks (lat/long/time series; speed; heading)
- route geometry (corridors, stopover clusters, tortuosity)
- timing metrics (departure date, arrival date, stopover duration)
- distance metrics (total path length; straight-line displacement)
- population synchrony (variance across individuals; cohort-level phase locking)

### C) Environmental coupling (shared external constraints)
- photoperiod (day length), irradiance, temperature, precipitation
- geomagnetic indices or field model parameters (where relevant)
- resource proxies (chlorophyll-a for marine, NDVI for terrestrial)
- ocean currents / winds (vectors; seasonality)

### D) Institutional replication requirements
- independent observers/instruments can re-measure the same variables
- protocols publish metadata (sampling intervals, sensor type, error bounds)
- uncertainty is quantifiable (GPS error, Argos class error, detection bias)

## Quantitative Rhythm Parameters (Measured Ranges)

### 1) Circadian timing (humans and animals)
Representative measured ranges (species-dependent; lab protocols vary):

| Parameter | Representative Range | Measurement Context |
|---|---:|---|
| circadian period (entrained) | ~24 h | field/lab under stable light-dark cycles |
| free-running period (constant conditions) | ~23.5‚Äì24.7 h | constant dim light / constant darkness; species dependent |
| daily phase variability within individuals | minutes ‚Üí ~1 h | depends on cue stability and measurement type |
| phase shift response to light (acute) | ~0.5‚Äì2 h per session | bright light exposure; protocol dependent |
| phase response curve (PRC) peak shift windows | early night delay / late night advance | classic PRC structure across taxa |

**Operational œÑ anchors (explicit examples):**
- A 60‚Äì120 minute bright-light exposure can measurably shift circadian phase the same day or next day (lab protocols).
- Seasonal entrainment shifts (circannual) occur over **weeks to months**, measurable as changes in migration onset, breeding onset, or molt timing.

## International Dataset Index (Primary LDSF Anchors)

### 1) Global occurrence & distribution infrastructure (broad, multi-taxa)
- **GBIF** ‚Äî occurrence records for migratory taxa (presence, time, location).
- **OBIS** ‚Äî marine species occurrences (including migratory marine species).

**What they provide (measurable variables):**
- occurrence lat/long, time stamps, observation/collection basis
- range shifts over decades (diachronic occurrence analysis)
- cross-institution replication (many contributing nodes)

---

### 2) High-resolution movement tracking (individual trajectories)
- **Movebank** ‚Äî animal tracking repository (GPS, Argos, biologgers) across taxa.
- **ARGOS** satellite telemetry datasets (where shared via projects/repositories).
- **Ocean Tracking Network (OTN)** ‚Äî acoustic telemetry and movement detections (marine).
- **IMOS** (Australia) and other national ocean observing systems (regional but rigorous).

**What they provide:**
- track time series (location/time), sampling rate (seconds ‚Üí hours), sensor metadata
- error models (GPS error, Argos location class uncertainty)
- stopover detection, corridor mapping, speed/heading distributions

---

### 3) Migration monitoring at population scale (birds especially; but extensible)
- **eBird** ‚Äî large-scale citizen-science observations with validation workflows.
- **European bird portals / national atlases** (regionally standardized monitoring).
- **BTO** and national ringing/banding programs (mark‚Äìrecapture, recoveries).

**What they provide:**
- phenology curves (first arrival, peak passage windows, departure timing)
- abundance indices across weeks/months (seasonal migration waves)
- long-term trend series enabling decade-scale replication tests

---

### 4) Chronobiology and rhythmic physiology (lab + field)
Sources vary by species, but the measurable category is stable:

- controlled circadian studies (actigraphy, wheel-running, melatonin/cortisol timing)
- field chronobiology (light loggers, temperature loggers, timing of activity onset)
- genomic/proteomic timing where available (time-series expression)

**What they provide:**
- period estimates under entrained and free-running conditions
- phase response measurements (light pulses; feeding schedules)
- rhythm amplitude and stability metrics across cohorts

---

### 5) Environmental ‚ÄúZeitgeber‚Äù datasets (external entrainment fields)
These are critical because they are **non-biological external constraints**:

- **Photoperiod / solar geometry**: computed from location/date (astronomical rules).
- **Climate time series**: temperature, precipitation, winds (national + global reanalysis).
- **Oceanography**: SST, currents, chlorophyll-a (satellite + buoy networks).
- **Geomagnetic field models / indices**: external orientation constraints where tested.
- **Tide predictions / observed tides**: coastal timing anchors.

**What they provide:**
- independent drivers to test coupling: ‚Äúmigration timing vs. photoperiod/temperature/current‚Äù
- external replication: any lab can pull the same environmental time series

## Reproducibility & Bias Controls (Publish-Ready Requirements)

To keep this ‚Äúpeer-stress-testable,‚Äù the following controls should be included (or explicitly marked absent) in any analysis using these datasets:

1. **Sampling interval declared** (e.g., 5 min GPS vs 6 h Argos).
2. **Location error model stated** (GPS error, Argos class).
3. **Detection bias handled** for citizen-science datasets (effort covariates, occupancy models).
4. **Season definitions standardized** (calendar vs phenological season).
5. **Time zone normalization** (UTC vs local solar time; daylight savings handling).
6. **Cohort definitions explicit** (age class, sex, population, tagging year).
7. **Cross-validation** across years and/or regions (no single-year claims).

## Falsifiability Statement (Non-Negotiable)

This LDSF classification must be revised or rejected if **any** of the following holds:

1. **Rhythms cannot be externally measured** with reproducible period/phase estimates across independent labs or field observers.
2. **Migration timing fails replication** across cohorts/years once measurement error and sampling bias are accounted for.
3. **Population synchrony is not measurable** (i.e., variance does not converge under improved sampling, or is observer-defined only).
4. Apparent shared patterns collapse into **non-comparable individual narratives** without stable population-level statistics.
5. External entrainment variables (photoperiod/temperature/tides/currents) show **no measurable coupling** where coupling is predicted by the measured-domain claim.

No retroactive reinterpretation is permitted.
